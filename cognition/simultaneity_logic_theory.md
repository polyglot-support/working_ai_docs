## Simultaneity-Resolved Logic and Anti-Set Foundations

### Abstract

This document introduces a formal foundation for simultaneity-resolved logic and anti-set theory. It attempts to reconstruct foundational logic structures to accommodate paradoxical, incoherent, and simultaneity-based phenomena which classical logic fails to resolve.

It draws from physical, epistemic, and set-theoretic extensions, aiming to unify discrete and continuous reasoning under a multi-valued recursive logical substrate. Particular emphasis is placed on the formal incorporation of simultaneity into logical reasoning and the application of anti-set structures to enable reversibility, nilpotency, and multitemporal symmetry.

---

### 1. Introduction

Traditional logic systems presuppose consistent, temporally ordered propositions. However, simultaneous or recursive causality—common in physical and abstract systems—violates these assumptions. To resolve this, we propose:

- A formal simultaneity-resolved logic
- Anti-set structures for inverse causal and set relations
- Resolution scaffolds for paradoxical constraints

This framework is intended to bridge foundational logic with physical systems where simultaneity and recursion are not edge cases, but dominant modes.

---

### 2. The Problem of Simultaneity in Classical Logic

Classical logic cannot represent two mutually contradictory propositions as true, nor can it represent mutually simultaneous events in ways that preserve causal integrity without violating temporal order. Examples include:

- Quantum entanglement
- Feedback in dynamical systems
- Observer-dependent truth conditions

Standard true/false evaluation collapses under these conditions.

### 3. Anti-Set Foundations

We define an **anti-set** \( \bar{A} \) as a set that contains the inverse or anti-correlated elements to a given set \( A \). Properties:

- \( A \cup \bar{A} = \emptyset \) under resolution closure
- Anti-sets may cancel or defer membership
- Useful in defining inverse participation, retrocausality

Applications include unbinding in causality graphs, symmetry of constraint graphs, and representing forbidden states.

### 4. Logical Nilpotency and Recursive Reversibility

We define a logic operator \( \mathcal{N} \) such that:

\[ \mathcal{N}(x) = \neg x \quad \text{and} \quad \mathcal{N}(\mathcal{N}(x)) = 0 \]

That is, negating twice yields nil, not truth. This enables representation of ephemeral, temporal, or context-dependent truths. Recursively:

\[ \mathcal{R}^n(x) = x \Rightarrow \text{Stable} \quad \text{else} \quad \text{Unstable or paradox-resolving} \]

### 5. Simultaneity Operators

We introduce the simultaneity operator \( \Sigma \), defined as:

\[ \Sigma(a, b) = \{a \leftrightarrow b\}_{t=0} \]

Interpreted as: "a and b are logically entangled at shared causal moment."

Under resolution:

- \( \Sigma(a, b) \Rightarrow a \oplus b = 0 \)
- Used to encode mutual cancellation or enforced paradox
